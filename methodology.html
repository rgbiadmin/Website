Methodology

How RGBI builds

The difference between a reporting tool and an intelligence system is not the software. It is the rigour applied at every decision point between a source transaction and a board-level number.

RGBI builds a governed measure hierarchy — not a flat collection of calculations. A layered computational architecture separates base metrics from board-level narrative, engineered to withstand audit scrutiny. Where most practitioners offer pivot tables and VLOOKUPs, RGBI delivers a semantic model that holds its integrity under executive challenge, regulatory review, and organisational change.

Principle 01

Governance is a design requirement, not documentation overhead

Most analytics teams treat documentation as something done after delivery — a record of what was built, created under time pressure and likely incomplete from day one. RGBI treats governance metadata as a first-class element of the semantic model itself.

Every measure is authored with its data owner, data steward, business definition, and computational lineage embedded at the point of creation. Every table has an agreed grain. Every relationship has a documented cardinality and direction. This is not a naming convention — it is an architectural standard.

The practical consequence is that any stakeholder — auditor, executive, or replacement analyst — can open the model and understand exactly what a measure computes, where its data originates, and who is accountable for its accuracy. No institutional memory required.

Principle 02

Thresholds are derived, not decided

The standard approach to performance monitoring is for a team to agree a threshold in a workshop — "red if below 95%, amber if below 98%" — and then implement it. The threshold has no statistical basis. It reflects the opinion of whoever was most confident in the room.

RGBI derives performance thresholds from the data's own statistical behaviour. Control limits are calculated from the process mean and standard deviation, expressed as sigma multiples that reflect the organisation's appetite for false positive alerts. Poisson modelling is applied where event rates are low and binomial approximation would be inappropriate.

The result is a monitoring system where an alert is statistically meaningful. It distinguishes common-cause variation — the normal noise of the process — from assignable causes that warrant operational response. Teams stop chasing noise and start responding to signal.

Principle 03

Filter context is stated, not assumed

DAX is a context-sensitive evaluation engine. The same formula returns different values depending on the filter context in which it is evaluated — and that context can be modified by slicers, visual dimensions, relationships, and explicit CALCULATE modifiers simultaneously. Practitioners who do not understand this build measures that produce the right answer in testing and the wrong answer in production.

Every RGBI measure is authored with explicit filter context documentation. ALLSELECTED and ALL are never treated as interchangeable. Row context and filter context are distinguished at the design stage, not discovered at the debugging stage. Edge cases — matrix column totals, bidirectional relationship interactions, many-to-many resolution — are identified and tested before delivery.

This is not excessive caution. It is the minimum required to build a measure that a stakeholder can trust when the report layout changes.

Principle 04

Base metrics and board narratives are architecturally separated

A common failure pattern in Power BI development is the flat measure library — all calculations at the same level of abstraction, mixed together without separation between raw transaction counts, domain aggregations, and executive summaries. When the source data changes, every dependent measure must be found and updated. When a business definition changes, the impact is unclear.

RGBI's approach separates computation into distinct, dependent layers. Base metrics sit at the foundation — direct calculations against source grain. Domain-level aggregations reference the base layer. Executive narrative measures reference the domain layer. Each layer is testable independently. A change at the base propagates correctly upward rather than requiring manual updates at every level.

This architecture also enforces a discipline that most analytics teams do not have: the number on a board slide is traceable, through the model, to a source transaction. That traceability is what separates an auditable intelligence system from a dashboard.

Principle 05

Knowledge transfer is a delivery requirement

An organisation that cannot maintain, extend, or interrogate a model without the original developer is not better off for having the model. Dependency on a single external practitioner is a governance risk — and one that RGBI treats as a design failure, not an acceptable outcome.

Every engagement includes structured knowledge transfer as a delivery requirement, not an optional add-on. Internal teams receive documentation sufficient to understand the model's architecture, the governance standards applied, and the conventions used throughout the measure library. Where appropriate, workshops and desk reviews ensure the capability exists within the client organisation to continue developing the model at the same standard.

The goal of every RGBI engagement is client independence after handover — not client dependency.

Standards & Frameworks

What underpins RGBI's approach

DAMA DMBOK 2.0

The Data Management Body of Knowledge — the international standard for data governance practice. RGBI's Platinum-tier implementation covers all relevant knowledge areas: metadata, data quality, data architecture, and master data management.

Statistical Process Control

Control chart methodology originating in Shewhart and Deming's work on process quality. Applied by RGBI to operational performance monitoring — bringing manufacturing-grade statistical rigour to data analytics.

Bayesian Inference

Where frequentist SPC is insufficient — typically where sample sizes are small or prior knowledge is strong — RGBI applies Bayesian methods to incorporate prior distributions and update performance estimates correctly.
